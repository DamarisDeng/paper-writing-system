# Speaker Notes — NIH Grant Termination Study Presentation

**Total Duration:** ~15-18 minutes
**Total Slides:** 13 slides
**Time per slide:** ~45-60 seconds

---

## Slide 1: Title (30 seconds)

**Opening:**
"Good morning/afternoon. I'll be presenting our recent analysis of NIH grant termination patterns during the 2025 federal funding actions, published in JAMA Network Open."

**Core Assertion:**
This study examines whether training grants—those supporting early-career researchers—were disproportionately targeted for termination compared to standard research grants.

**Transition:**
"Let me start with the key question that motivated this research."

---

## Slide 2: Research Question (45 seconds)

**Opening:**
"Our research question was straightforward: Were NIH research training grants disproportionately terminated in 2025 compared with research and development grants?"

**Evidence:**
"We focused on this question because early reports suggested training grants were particularly vulnerable. Training grants include F-series fellowships for pre- and postdoctoral researchers, T32 institutional training grants, and K-series career development awards. These represent the primary pipeline for training the next generation of biomedical investigators."

**Why This Matters:**
"The 2025 federal funding actions affected over $17 billion in NIH research awards. If training grants were being selectively terminated, the impact on scientific workforce diversity and early-career researchers could be devastating."

**Transition:**
"To answer this question, we analyzed data from the NIH Grant Witness dataset. Let me explain our study design."

---

## Slide 3: Study Overview (60 seconds)

**Opening:**
"We conducted a cross-sectional analysis using the NIH Grant Witness termination dataset—a publicly available registry of NIH awards affected by the 2025 federal funding actions."

**Evidence:**
"Our final analytic sample comprised 5,219 NIH grants with definitive outcomes—either terminated or reinstated. We excluded grants still frozen, those from minor funding categories like Small Business and Construction, and records with missing award data."

"We compared two groups: research training and career development grants versus research and development grants. Training grants included F31, F32, F30 fellowships, T32 institutional training grants, and K01, K08, K23, and K99 career development awards. R&D grants included the standard R01, R21, R03, and R56 mechanisms."

**Transition:**
"Now, let's look at what we found in the unadjusted analysis."

---

## Slide 4: Unadjusted Findings (45 seconds)

**Opening:**
"When we first looked at the raw termination rates, we saw what appeared to be a striking disparity."

**Evidence:**
"Training grants had a 30.1% termination rate compared to just 16.2% for R&D grants—a crude difference of 14 percentage points. On the surface, this suggested training grants were nearly twice as likely to be terminated."

"This finding aligned with early reports and concerns about disproportionate harm to training programs."

**Transition:**
"However, raw numbers can be misleading. The key question is: did this disparity persist after accounting for other factors? Let's look at the adjusted analysis."

---

## Slide 5: Adjusted Analysis (60 seconds)

**Opening:**
"When we ran a multivariable logistic regression adjusting for institutional type, geographic region, and award amount, the story changed dramatically."

**Evidence:**
"After adjustment, training grant status was no longer significantly associated with termination. The odds ratio was 1.11 with a 95% confidence interval of 0.90 to 1.36 and a P-value of .33."

"This represents a substantial attenuation from the unadjusted association. The confidence interval crosses 1, meaning we cannot reject the null hypothesis of no association."

**Key Point:**
"The crude 14-percentage-point difference between training and R&D grants was largely explained by confounding factors—specifically, where training grants were located and what types of institutions hosted them."

**Transition:**
"So what were these confounding factors? Let's look at the strongest predictors in our model."

---

## Slide 6: Geographic Region (60 seconds)

**Opening:**
"The most striking finding in our analysis was the extraordinary geographic variation in termination rates."

**Evidence:**
"Grants from institutions in Southern states had 25 times higher odds of termination compared to grants from Northeastern institutions. This is an enormous effect—an odds ratio of 25.23 with a very tight confidence interval."

"Midwestern institutions also faced elevated risk at 2.5 times higher than the Northeast. Western institutions showed no significant difference from the Northeast."

**Key Insight:**
"Training grants are disproportionately concentrated in Southern states. When we account for this geographic concentration, the apparent training grant disadvantage disappears. The crude association was confounded by geography."

**Transition:**
"Beyond geography, institutional type also played a major role."

---

## Slide 7: Institutional Type (60 seconds)

**Opening:**
"The second major predictor was institutional type. Hospital and non-profit institutions faced dramatically higher termination odds compared to medical schools."

**Evidence:**
"Hospital and non-profit institution grants had 10.5-fold higher odds of termination compared with university medical school grants. This was the strongest institutional predictor in our model."

"University-Other settings showed a borderline elevated odds ratio of 1.22, but this was just barely non-significant with a P-value of .051."

**Why This Matters:**
"Hospitals and non-profit research institutes typically have fewer legal and administrative resources than major research universities. They have less leverage to contest terminations and smaller grant portfolios—meaning even a single termination is highly disruptive."

**Transition:**
"There was one more important predictor: the size of the award."

---

## Slide 8: Award Size (45 seconds)

**Opening:**
"We also found that total award amount was independently and inversely associated with termination—larger grants were more likely to survive."

**Evidence:**
"Each unit increase in log-transformed total award was associated with a 34% reduction in termination odds. This translates to an odds ratio of 0.66."

"To put this in context: R&D grants had a median award of $2.1 million, while training grants had a median of just $274,000. This size difference likely contributed to the crude disparity."

**Interpretation:**
"Higher-value grants may receive preferential protection because they support more complex research programs, more investigators, and are more visible in institutional advocacy efforts."

**Transition:**
"We conducted several sensitivity analyses to test the robustness of our findings."

---

## Slide 9: Sensitivity Analyses (45 seconds)

**Opening:**
"We ran three pre-specified sensitivity analyses to verify our results."

**Evidence:**
"First, we stratified by institutional type. Within University-Medical institutions, the odds ratio for training grants was 1.22, not statistically significant. Within University-Other institutions, it was 1.17, also not significant."

"Second, we ran a model without geographic adjustment. Here, the training grant odds ratio became 1.24 with a P-value of .02—statistically significant. This confirms that geographic confounding explains the crude association."

"Third, we used state-level fixed effects for the 15 highest-frequency states. The odds ratio was 1.09 with a P-value of .45—consistent with our primary analysis."

**Transition:**
"All sensitivity analyses pointed to the same conclusion. Let me summarize our key findings."

---

## Slide 10: Summary of Findings (60 seconds)

**Opening:**
"Our study revealed three key findings."

**Evidence:**
"First, training grants were not independently targeted. After adjustment, the odds ratio was 1.11 with a P-value of .33—not statistically significant. The apparent disparity was explained by confounding."

"Second, geography was the primary driver. Southern institutions faced 25-fold higher termination odds compared to the Northeast. This regional pattern suggests structural targeting, not grant-level targeting."

"Third, institutional type mattered greatly. Hospital and non-profit institutions faced 10-fold higher odds of termination, likely due to fewer resources to contest decisions. Award size was also protective."

**Transition:**
"These findings have important implications for policy responses."

---

## Slide 11: Policy Implications (60 seconds)

**Opening:**
"Our findings suggest that equity-focused responses should target underresourced institutional and regional contexts, not specific grant mechanisms."

**Evidence:**
"If training grants were being targeted, remediation efforts should focus on protecting scientific training pathways. But our analysis shows that's not the case. The drivers are geographic and institutional."

"Policy responses should prioritize: hospitals and non-profit institutions, Southern states with the highest termination rates, and smaller award programs regardless of mechanism."

**Future Directions:**
"Future research should track longitudinal changes in grant status as reinstatement processes continue. We also need qualitative studies to understand institutional decision-making and advocacy capacity."

**Transition:**
"In conclusion..."

---

## Slide 12: Conclusions (45 seconds)

**Opening:**
"In this cross-sectional analysis of 5,219 NIH grants affected by the 2025 federal funding actions, we found that geographic region and institutional type—not grant funding category—were the primary structural predictors of permanent termination."

**Evidence:**
"Southern-state institutions and hospital and non-profit settings faced dramatically higher termination odds. Training grants themselves were not significantly associated with termination after adjustment."

**Bottom Line:**
"The 2025 NIH grant terminations were not about training versus research—they were about WHERE the research was happening and WHO was doing it. Equity-focused responses should target underresourced institutions and regions, irrespective of grant mechanism."

**Transition:**
"Thank you for your attention. I'm happy to take questions."

---

## Slide 13: Q&A (as needed)

**Opening:**
"Thank you. I'd be happy to answer any questions."

**Potential Questions & Talking Points:**

**Q: Why do you think Southern states had such high termination rates?**
A: "Multiple factors likely contribute: differential political environments, institutional capacity to mount legal challenges, the concentration of institutions that received court-ordered reinstatements in Northeastern states, and possible differences in grant subject matter. The Harvard lawsuit, for example, secured reinstatements for Massachusetts institutions."

**Q: Were training grants completely unaffected?**
A: "Not completely—they had a 30% termination rate versus 16% for R&D grants. But after accounting for where they were located and what institutions hosted them, the grant mechanism itself wasn't the driver. The disparity was explained by confounding."

**Q: What should policymakers do differently?**
A: "Rather than focusing solely on protecting training grants, remediation efforts should target underresourced institutions and regions. This could include targeted funding opportunities, legal support for hospitals and non-profits, and prioritizing reinstatement for Southern institutions."

## Delivery Tips

1. **Pacing:** Aim for ~45-60 seconds per slide for the main content. The title and Q&A slides can be shorter.

2. **Emphasis:** When citing key statistics (OR = 25.23, OR = 10.52), pause briefly to let the numbers sink in.

3. **Visual Aids:** The figures from the paper are included in the slides. Reference them directly when speaking—e.g., "As shown in Figure 2..."

4. **Transitions:** Use the transition phrases provided to maintain flow between slides.

5. **Questions:** If you don't know the answer to a question, it's okay to say, "That's an important question we didn't address in this analysis—worthy of future research."

## Post-Presentation Discussion Points

If discussion continues, consider raising:

1. **Limitations:** Cross-sectional design captures single time point; some grants may change status; unmeasured confounders like grant subject matter.

2. **Broader Context:** How geographic inequality in research funding affects the diversity of the scientific enterprise.

3. **Future Work:** Need for longitudinal tracking and qualitative studies of institutional advocacy.

4. **Policy Action:** What specific remediation efforts would be most effective given these findings.

%% =============================================================================
%%  INTRODUCTION
%% =============================================================================
\section*{Introduction}

The COVID-19 pandemic caused more than 1 million deaths in the United States by 2022, with mortality disproportionately affecting certain regions and populations during the Delta variant wave of mid-2021.\textsuperscript{1} Healthcare workers (HCWs) were prioritized for COVID-19 vaccination starting in December 2020, and by mid-2021, vaccination coverage among HCWs varied substantially across states.\textsuperscript{2,3} To increase vaccination rates, 16 states and Washington, DC, implemented HCW COVID-19 vaccine mandates between July and August 2021.\textsuperscript{4}

Prior studies have demonstrated that COVID-19 vaccines are highly effective at preventing severe outcomes, including death, with effectiveness exceeding 90\% against hospitalization and death during the Delta period.\textsuperscript{5,6} Studies of nursing home staff mandates have shown increased vaccination coverage following policy implementation, but evidence on population-level mortality outcomes remains limited.\textsuperscript{7} Similarly, research on hospital system--level mandates has demonstrated improved staff vaccination rates, but state-level ecological studies are lacking.\textsuperscript{8}

The association between state-level HCW vaccine mandates and COVID-19 mortality at the population level has not been adequately studied. Understanding this relationship is critical for informing public health policy, particularly as new variants emerge and booster vaccination strategies are considered. This study aimed to evaluate the association between state-level HCW COVID-19 vaccine mandates and COVID-19 mortality rates during the Delta variant period of July to October 2021.


%% =============================================================================
%%  METHODS
%% =============================================================================
\section*{Methods}

\subsection*{Data}
This study used publicly available, deidentified data and was exempt from institutional review board approval, following the STROBE reporting guideline for observational studies.\textsuperscript{9} State-level COVID-19 mortality data were obtained from the New York Times COVID-19 repository, which aggregates data from state and local health departments.\textsuperscript{10} Population estimates for 2021 were obtained from the US Census Bureau Population Estimates Program.\textsuperscript{11} HCW vaccine mandate data were compiled from state policy records, including announcement date, scope, and presence of test-out options.

The study period was July 1 to October 31, 2021, corresponding to the 12 weeks following the first HCW mandate announcements and encompassing the peak of the Delta variant wave in the United States. All 50 states and Washington, DC were included in analyses (n=52 jurisdictions).

\subsection*{Outcome Measures}
The primary outcome was state-level COVID-19 mortality rate, calculated as cumulative COVID-19 deaths during the study period divided by the 2021 state population, expressed per 100,000 population. Deaths were attributed to the state where they occurred. Secondary outcomes included COVID-19 case rates per 100,000 population, calculated analogously.

\subsection*{Exposure}
The primary exposure was state-level HCW vaccine mandate status, defined as a binary indicator (1 if the state implemented a HCW vaccine mandate announced between July 1 and August 31, 2021; 0 otherwise). Mandate characteristics included scope (healthcare facilities only vs including congregate settings), presence of test-out option (yes/no), and timing (July vs August announcement).

\subsection*{Covariates}
Covariates included Census region (Northeast, Midwest, South, West) and state population (log\textsubscript{10}-transformed for modeling). Region was included to account for geographic variation in COVID-19 timing and severity.

\subsection*{Statistical Analysis}
Descriptive statistics summarized state characteristics by mandate status. Welch t-tests compared continuous variables, and chi-square tests compared categorical variables. Linear regression models estimated the association between mandate status and mortality rate. Three models were fitted: unadjusted (mandate only); adjusted for region; and fully adjusted (region and log\textsubscript{10} population). Sensitivity analyses examined associations among mandate states by test-out option status and announcement timing.

Analyses were performed using Python version 3.12 with pandas and numpy. Statistical significance was set at 2-sided P < .05. No adjustment for multiple comparisons was prespecified.


%% =============================================================================
%%  RESULTS
%% =============================================================================
\section*{Results}

The study included 52 US jurisdictions. Sixteen (30.8\%) implemented HCW vaccine mandates during the study period (Table 1). Mandate states had larger mean populations (8.4 million [SD, 9.8] vs 5.6 million [SD, 5.9]) and were disproportionately located in the Northeast region (37.5\% vs 8.3\% of non-mandate states). Overall mortality during the study period was 41.6 deaths per 100,000 (SD, 24.9), with substantial variation by mandate status (mandate: 22.7 [SD, 9.6]; non-mandate: 49.9 [SD, 25.1]; P < .001).

In unadjusted analysis, mandates were associated with 27.3 fewer deaths per 100,000 (95\% CI, --14.5 to --40.0; P < .001) (Table 2). After adjustment for region, the association was --22.4 (95\% CI, --11.0 to --33.9; P < .001). In fully adjusted models including region and population, mandates were associated with 23.4 fewer deaths per 100,000 (95\% CI, --11.5 to --35.2; P < .001) (Figure 1). The model R\textsuperscript{2} was 0.59, indicating that 59\% of variance in state mortality was explained by mandate status, region, and population.

Geographic variation in mortality was evident (Figure 2). Southern states had the highest mean mortality (64.4 per 100,000 [SD, 27.7]), whereas Northeastern states had the lowest (24.6 per 100,000 [SD, 12.6]). The association between mandates and lower mortality was consistent across sensitivity analyses. Among mandate states, those with test-out options (n=5) had numerically higher mortality than those without (25.8 vs 21.1 per 100,000), although this difference was not statistically significant (difference, 3.8 [95\% CI, --6.5 to 14.1]; P = .45). Early adopters (July announcements, n=2) had lower mortality than later adopters (August, n=14), but this difference was also not statistically significant (difference, --5.2 [95\% CI, --19.7 to 9.3]; P = .46).

%% =============================================================================
%%  DISCUSSION
%% =============================================================================
\section*{Discussion}

This ecological study found that state-level HCW COVID-19 vaccine mandates implemented in July to August 2021 were associated with substantially lower COVID-19 mortality rates during the subsequent 12 weeks. After adjusting for region and population, mandate states experienced 23.4 fewer deaths per 100,000 population compared with non-mandate states. This association represents a relative reduction of approximately 47\% and remained statistically significant across multiple model specifications.

The magnitude of this association is consistent with prior studies demonstrating the effectiveness of HCW vaccination mandates at increasing coverage and reducing COVID-19 outcomes in healthcare settings.\textsuperscript{7,8} Mullen et al\textsuperscript{4} found that state mandates increased HCW vaccination coverage by approximately 5 percentage points, while Chen et al\textsuperscript{7} reported that nursing home mandates were associated with reduced resident mortality. The present study extends these findings to the state population level, suggesting that HCW mandates may have spillover benefits beyond healthcare facilities.

Several mechanisms may explain the observed association. First, vaccinated HCWs are less likely to contract and transmit COVID-19, reducing nosocomial infections.\textsuperscript{5,6} Second, HCW vaccination may reduce community transmission through household and social contacts. Third, states that implemented mandates may have had other public health measures (eg, masking policies, testing programs) that contributed to lower mortality. However, the robustness of the association after regional adjustment suggests that geographic confounding alone does not explain the findings.

The regional variation in mortality is notable and consistent with prior reports of the Delta wave's disproportionate impact on Southern states.\textsuperscript{1} The concentration of mandates in the Northeast and West may reflect both political factors and preexisting public health infrastructure. The lack of significant differences by test-out option or timing may be due to small sample sizes among mandate states.

\subsection*{Limitations}
This study has several limitations. First, the ecological design precludes causal inference at the individual level; findings apply to state populations, not individuals. Second, unmeasured confounding is likely---states that adopted mandates differ systematically from non-adopter states in ways beyond region and population (eg, vaccination rates, public health capacity, political environment). Third, the analysis does not account for other concurrent COVID-19 mitigation policies. Fourth, mandate status is based on announcement date, not implementation or enforcement dates. Fifth, outcome data are from passive surveillance and subject to reporting variation. Finally, the small number of mandate states limits power for subgroup analyses.

Despite these limitations, the study provides evidence of an association between HCW vaccine mandates and lower population mortality during a critical period of the pandemic. Future research using individual-level data and controlling for a broader set of confounders would strengthen causal inference. Additionally, studies examining the longer-term effects of mandates and their impact on subsequent variants are warranted.


%% =============================================================================
%%  CONCLUSIONS
%% =============================================================================
\section*{Conclusions}

State-level HCW COVID-19 vaccine mandates were associated with substantially lower COVID-19 mortality rates during the Delta variant period. These findings suggest that HCW vaccination policies may confer population-level benefits and support the consideration of such policies during future infectious disease emergencies.
